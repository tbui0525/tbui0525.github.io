Another thing one must consider are Nan values. In the ocean data, since water does not cover the entire globe, there will be Nan data points (or points that are land instead of water). NOAA formats these Nans as the value $-9.9 \times 10^{36}$. We do not want this number in our calculations because the temperature of the water at that point is not $-9.9 \times 10^{36} K$, but nonexistant.¶

Standardizing and Normalizing Data¶

Typically, data is normalized and standardized before further processing. For the prupose of this particular computation, it eliminates the significance of the units the data is measured in. Whether the data was measured in Kelvin, Centigrade, Fahrenheit, etc. the resulting thermal patterns post-computation should be the same.¶

Mathematically, standardized and mean-centered data holds much more information than the raw data or raw anomalies. The raw data does not provide much other than the temperature of that spot in that point in time. The anomalies provide whether the temperature is relatively high or low for that moment in time, but not the extremity of the thermal difference. The standardized and normalized data provide a z-score which provides not only whether the temperature is higher or lower than the typical value, but the extremity of the anomaly.¶
